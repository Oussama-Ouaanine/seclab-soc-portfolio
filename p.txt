The survey looks well-structured overall! Here are my thoughts:

**Strengths**

The form is clean and logically sequenced — profiling first, then problem validation, then idea testing. That's the right order for market validation. Questions 5 and 7 are particularly strong: Q5 surfaces real pain points, and Q7 is a direct test of the value proposition.

**Areas to improve**

**Q4 and Q5 are both single-select where they should allow multiple selections.** Many organizations use hybrid approaches (e.g., basic logs *and* some open-source tools). Forcing one choice loses nuance.

**Q7 is slightly leading.** Describing the solution as "plug-and-play" and mentioning "saves time and money" in the answer options biases respondents toward yes. A more neutral framing would be: *"Would this address a real pain point for your team?"* with plain options like Yes / Somewhat / No.

**Q8 has a problem.** The third option ("not familiar enough") is likely to be the most common answer, which makes the data less actionable. Consider replacing it with: *"I'd want to evaluate them before trusting them"* — that's more useful signal.

**Q6 mixes two things.** It asks about compliance *motivation* but the checkboxes are frameworks. A respondent subject to GDPR might still not see compliance as their *primary* driver. Consider splitting: first ask *"Is compliance a major driver for you?"* (Yes/No/Somewhat), then ask *"Which frameworks apply to you?"*

**Minor notes**

- Q10 asking for email in a Google Form works, but many people skip it due to spam concerns. A checkbox like *"Yes, contact me when the MVP is ready"* might get more responses, then follow up separately.
- The form is bilingual in the document but make sure the actual Google Form is served as two separate forms, not mixed — alternating languages in one form looks unprofessional.

Overall it's a solid foundation for an academic market validation exercise. The main fix is neutralizing Q7 and making Q4 multi-select.
